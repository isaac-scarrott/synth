# 2026-07-22

## Synth 0.8.0 shipped (build 303)

Minor bump for the new liveness indicator: a working or running session now shows a cyan
5×5 lattice beating outward from its centre, replacing the amber dot that shipped through
0.7.1. Notarized, stapled, and verified credential-less from the public bucket (spctl
accepted / Notarized Developer ID, staple valid, appcast newest `Synth-0.8.0.zip` at
`sparkle:version` 303 with `edSignature` on all 18 enclosures). Users on 0.7.1 take a 697KB
delta rather than the 137MB download.

**Merge note.** `v0.7.1` had been tagged and published from `crash-reporting-mach-port-ordering`
without ever landing on main, so main was missing two shipped crash fixes (the Bluetooth/TCC
abort and Breakpad swallowing crashes) while carrying a *higher* build number. Releasing from
main as-is would have pushed every installed copy onto a build where both crashes returned —
Sparkle orders by `CFBundleVersion`, and 303 > 296, so the regression would have auto-installed.
The tag was merged into main first, making main a superset of everything published; the shipped
bundle carries `NSBluetoothAlwaysUsageDescription`, which is the check that proves it. A release
cut from a branch that never merges back leaves this trap armed for whoever releases next.

## Synth installs from a disk image (0.8.1, build 307)

The download was a zip, which hands someone an app and no place to put it. People ran Synth
straight out of `~/Downloads`, where macOS app translocation executes it read-only from a
randomised mount point — and Synth, which locates its bundled `synth-hook` relative to its own
bundle, cannot find its own tooling there. A first-time installer on another Mac also hit
"Apple could not verify Synth is free of malware", the message Sequoia shows when Gatekeeper
cannot complete its check.

`release.sh` now also builds a `.dmg` holding the app beside an `/Applications` symlink, so the
install is a drag and the app ends up somewhere translocation does not apply. Two properties of
it are load-bearing:

- **It is cut from the already-stapled app, and notarized again in its own right.** A copy
  dragged out of an image carries no trace of the image it came from. Staple only the image and
  the app inside verifies solely while Apple is reachable, failing offline as exactly the
  malware-verification message this set out to fix. Both artifacts therefore carry their own
  ticket, at the cost of a second notarization wait per release.
- **It is built into `build/`, never `app/releases/`.** `generate_appcast` treats every archive
  in that directory as a release enclosure and would have published the image as a second,
  competing update for the same version.

Sparkle's path is untouched: the updater still fetches `Synth-<version>.zip` plus deltas, and
the `Synth.zip` alias stays published because shipped builds reference it. The two artifacts now
have two distinct audiences — people get the image, installed copies get the zip.

Verified as an outsider rather than by exit code: anonymous download of the public `Synth.dmg`
with Safari's quarantine flag set, image assessed `accepted / Notarized Developer ID`, mounted,
and the app *copied out and the image unmounted* before checking it — `codesign --deep --strict`
clean, staple valid, `spctl` accepted standalone, which is the state a real install is in.
Appcast newest `Synth-0.8.1.zip` at `sparkle:version` 307 with `edSignature`; 0.8.0 users take a
295KB delta.

**Site note.** `landing/index.html` is the source, but the deployed site is a separate public
repo (`isaac-scarrott/synth-site`, Pages from root) and nothing automates the copy, so edits here
reached nobody until someone made the hop by hand. The release skill now documents it, including
the ordering: publish the release first, or the download button points at a bucket object that
does not exist yet.

## The release tag names the commit that was built

`release.sh` tagged at the end of its run, reading `HEAD` at that moment. A release spends ~20
minutes waiting on Apple, and a commit made in that window silently moved the tag onto a commit
that was never built — which is what happened to `v0.8.1` on its first push: the app was built
from `Synth 0.8.1` (`git rev-list --count` = 307, the `CFBundleVersion` actually shipped) while
the tag landed on the docs commit made during the wait, counting 308. Rebuilding from the tag
would have produced a build number no release ever had.

The commit is now pinned next to the clean-tree guard, before anything is built, and the tag is
created against that. `v0.8.1` was moved back to the commit it was cut from; the shipped binary
was never in question, since the stray commit touched only the release skill's documentation and
nothing that enters the bundle.

## The liveness indicator is a sphere, in violet

The 5×5 cyan lattice had two problems, one of colour and one of shape.

**Colour.** Cyan sat close enough to the slate-blue needs-input `?` that at 14px, down a column
of right-side indicators, the two read as the same signal. Every other hue on that axis is spoken
for — the `?` is blue, the `!` red, the checked-out dot blue, the AI mark copper — so liveness
took violet, which nothing else claims. It is now two tokens rather than one: `--live-lift`
(`#c4b5fd` / `#ede9fe`) and `--live-deep` (`#4c1d95` / `#6d28d9`), the two ends of a ramp.

**Shape.** A square of square cells, for a thing whose whole job is to look alive. The lattice
keeps its footprint and its 25-cell markup, but the four corners lie outside the ball and go
unpainted, so the silhouette reads round; the remaining 21 dots are circles that shrink from
2.5px at the centre to 1.35px at the rim and sink along the lift→deep ramp as they go out. Both
cues say the same thing — the middle is the sphere's near face, the rim is where the surface
turns away from you. Depth from geometry, not from a drop shadow.

The beat survives intact, because the beat was never the problem: one clock, the pulse leaving
the centre and travelling out. What changed is the metric. Delay was Manhattan distance × 130ms,
which makes a *diamond* wavefront — invisible on a square, and at odds with a round mark once the
corners went. It is now true radial distance × 118ms, so the wave leaves the middle as a circle.

Ported to SwiftUI in the same pass. Two details the port could not copy: rings are keyed by
*squared* distance, an integer, so a cell lands on its ring exactly rather than by float equality
on `sqrt`; and macOS 14 has no `Color.mix`, so the three intermediate stops of the ramp are the
oklab mixes precomputed and committed as constants, where CSS computes them at paint time.

Rejected along the way: a ring-wavefront orb, an orbit sweep, and a foreshortened projection
where dots crowd toward the rim (physically correct, unreadable at 14px). Of ten colour
treatments — iridescent, banded, thermal, glass beads, core bloom among them — the plain radial
depth ramp won, because at this size anything cleverer is mush.

## The browser MCP server survives a loaded engine

Field report from two lanes of local testing: one Synth engine holding ~35 CDP targets across
several agents' sessions, and every browser tool — navigate, evaluate, snapshot, close — failing
with `browserType.connectOverCDP: Timeout 10000ms exceeded`. The servers under test were healthy
throughout. The tooling was the only thing that broke, and it broke opaquely enough that
diagnosing it cost more than the task it interrupted. Seven changes, in the order they bite.

**Attach cost scales with the engine, so the budget does too.** `connectOverCDP` enumerates every
target on the endpoint — all worktrees, all agents, the user's own tabs — so its cost is a
property of the machine, not of anything the calling agent owns. A fixed 10s budget therefore
converts "the box is busy" into "every tool is permanently broken". The budget is now
`targets × 1.5s`, floor 20s, ceiling 120s, with the count read first from `/json/list` (one cheap
HTTP request that answers even when the attach cannot finish). Measured on a 6-target engine mid-
work: 15.8s to attach. The old ceiling was 10.

**The timeout says what it knows.** It names the port, the elapsed budget, the target count, and
the fact that attaching enumerates every target — so the number points at engine-wide load rather
than at a tab of yours. The original message sent the reporter hunting a wedged page for a long
time; they eventually proved by hand, over raw CDP, that all 35 targets attached fine.

**Session→page mapping is cached and probed concurrently.** Mapping a page to its Synth session
means evaluating `window.__synthSessionId` on it, at a 2s timeout. That ran serially over every
page, on every tool call: 35 stalled targets cost 70s before any work started, which is why the
tools got slower the longer a session ran. Probes now run in parallel and successful answers are
cached per page for its life (the CEF shim restamps the same id on every main-frame load, so the
answer cannot change). Only truthy answers cache — a page that hasn't loaded yet reports null,
and that must not stick.

**Reconnects are gated on evidence.** A lookup miss used to retire the connection and dial again,
because CEF emits no attach events for targets created since the client connected. Under load
that dial times out, the cache is left empty, and every subsequent call pays a fresh attach — the
death spiral the report describes. The endpoint's own target list now settles it: when it shows
nothing Playwright can't already see, the miss is real and the reconnect is skipped.

**A navigation that ran out of time is not a navigation that failed.** `browser_navigate` waited
for `load` with a 20s cap, which a cold Next.js route compile does not survive. It reported an
error for navigations that had already landed — and because the URL was a single-use handoff
code, the "failure" silently consumed it. Three launch codes went that way. The default is now
`domcontentloaded` at 30s, both configurable, and a timeout returns a non-error result saying the
request was sent, where the page actually is, and not to retry a single-use URL.

**One source of truth per fact.** `browser_list` reported Synth's copy of a session's url, which
lags a navigation in flight; the reporter trusted it over CDP and concluded a launch had been
rejected when it simply hadn't resolved. The url is now read through to the live page, and a
session with no live target is marked `lastKnownUrl` with a note rather than quietly served stale.

**Partial states get rolled back.** `browser_create` used to return `{sessionId, warning: "its CDP
target never appeared within 15s"}` — a row in the sidebar with no usable page, left for the user
to clear. It now closes the session and errors, so the call yields a working browser or leaves
nothing behind. `browser_close` used to report success on the app's word alone; it now holds the
page across the close, waits for the target to go, force-closes a lingering one, and only errors
if that fails too. A "closed" that leaves a live tab is worse than an error.

**New: `browser_health`, and worktree scoping.** There was no health check, no target count, no
recovery step — the only way out of a wedged engine was a hand-written websocket client. Worse,
the absence of a supported answer to "which process is this target, and is it alive" is what
drove the reporter to map targets to PIDs by making 27 tabs allocate 300MB each, retaining ~8GB,
driving swap to 6GB and load average to 31 on a machine other people were working on. Assume
agents will do invasive things when the safe path doesn't exist. `browser_health` reports the
engine's target count, the current attach budget and how long the last attach took, and probes
each of this worktree's sessions for responsiveness; `reconnect: true` drops the cached
connection and dials again. Separately, every target lookup now goes through the app's list of
*this worktree's* sessions, so an agent can no longer drive, or trip over, a browser belonging to
another worktree on the shared engine — close was already owner-gated, driving was not.

**Also: `browser_cookies`, and scoped snapshots.** A valid handoff session obtained by curl (an
HttpOnly cookie JavaScript cannot write) could not be transplanted into the browser, so a working
session was unusable; `browser_cookies` gets and sets them over `Network.getCookies` /
`setCookies`. And `browser_snapshot` — which returned 379,838 characters for one product listing
and then advised narrowing a query it offered no way to narrow — takes `selector` and `maxDepth`.

Two gaps left standing, deliberately. Per-worktree browser engines would contain the blast radius
properly, rather than scoping lookups within one shared engine; that is an app-side change, not
an MCP one. And `browser_health` reports responsiveness but not a target's PID — CDP exposes no
target→process mapping, so honestly reporting the gap beats inventing a probe for it.

## Every sidebar row ends in two buttons, not one ⋯

The hover-revealed ⋯ was the only affordance on a row, so the two things you actually do most —
delete this, add one of these — both cost a menu. Each row now reveals a pair on the same axis:
delete first, then the verb its level owns. A workspace and a worktree get **+** (new worktree,
new session), since they have children to add; a session leaf has none, so it keeps **⋯**, which
still opens ⌘K pinned to that row where its rename / close / browser verbs live. Delete goes
through the same ⌘K confirm frame as ⌫, so no button destroys anything on one click.

The dots also read too small to aim at — the button box went 20px→22px, the glyph 13px→16px for
the dots and 14px for the trash and plus.

Two buttons are wider than the 24px indicator axis the old ⋯ sat on, so long labels ran under
them. Nothing moves to make room — the row's boxes are identical hovered or not; the label simply
stops painting where the cluster starts, masked out over a 14px ramp, the same way the status
metadata under the buttons only fades. Fixing that surfaced a dangling selector list — the menu-open half
of the metadata fade had been swallowed into `.menu__btn`'s rule, so a row with its ⌘K frame
pinned kept its metadata visible *and* handed it button padding. The rule is closed off now.

## Synth 0.9.1 shipped (build 320)

A single-fix patch, out the same day as 0.9.0 because the fix is a crash. Holding ⌘, ⌥, ⌃ or ⇧ on
its own killed the process: `flagsChanged` routed the modifier press through the same `sendKey`
path as a real keystroke, which reads `charactersIgnoringModifiers`, and AppKit raises on that
property for any non-key event. Chained shortcuts are the point of Synth, so the keys people rest
their fingers on were the ones taking the app down.

## The light terminal moves to cool near-white

The warm paper surface (`#f3efe7`) is gone; the light terminal is `#f7f8fa` carrying `--ink`
(`#1c1e23`) as its text, so the terminal and the chrome around it are the same ink rather than two
neighbouring greys.

The complaint that started this was Claude Code, and the palette turned out not to be the cause.
Claude Code never touches the terminal's 16 colours — it paints with its own hard-coded truecolor
theme, its default is `dark`, it does no background detection, and nothing sets `theme` in
`~/.claude.json`. Synth's light mode was therefore running Claude Code's *dark* theme, whose
`text` is `#ffffff`, on a cream background: 1.03:1. Tool names, todo items and body text were
simply absent while errors and warnings survived, which is why it read as "some things". Moving
to a cool near-white is the half Synth owns — Claude Code's light theme is built against `#fff`,
and warmth was costing it about 10% of every contrast ratio it was designed for. The other half
is a `theme` Synth has to set, and that is still open.

The 16 colours were rebuilt around the new surface rather than carried over. Each hue is placed by
searching lightness for an exact ratio, so the set is even instead of drifting between 4.5 and 5.2:
hues at 7:1, brights at 9:1, dim at 6.1:1, default text at 15.7:1. Brights deliberately stop at
9:1 — pushed as dark as they would go, the six hues collapse into each other and into black, and
the emphasis step stops carrying a hue at all.

Slots 7 and 15 stop being inverted. Making every bright *deeper* is right for text on a light
background, but 7 and 15 are not only text: they are what a TUI paints under white-on-colour, and
as near-black they took selected rows, inverse video and status bars to 1.04:1 — the worst numbers
anywhere in the theme, and invisible in fzf, lazygit and any picker. They are light again, which
puts those cells at 7.4:1. The cost is accepted and real: an app that prints explicit *white text*
on the light surface now reads faint. Well-behaved TUIs use the default foreground for body text,
and Solarized Light and GitHub Light both take the same trade.
